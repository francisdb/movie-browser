  • magnolia12883
    MCT:
    August 2, 2008
    Chris Carter's "The X-Files" was a TV phenomenon from 1993 to 2002. It went from the first Gulf War to the cusp of the second and spanned presidencies. Its government conspiracy storylines were convoluted at best and tangled webs of confusion at worst. Its monster of the week stories started in myth and horror classics and expanded to include cliches turned on their heads before devolving into cheap, lame graspings at straws.

    Unfortunately, ten years after the first film (which was a "mythology"-based conspiracy storyline carried over from the previous season of the series) and six years after the long-in-the-tooth series' fateful demise, this new movie is a monster of the week story (of sorts) that neither provides us with a clear, definitive monster nor the closure a second film should give.

    My main problems with this film are as follows:

    1) The villain's plot is not completely crystal clear even in the end when the explanation and requisite resolution are brought in to end the film on a note of... duration.
    2) It feels like a half-memorable episode from season 8 or 9 (minus those later seasons' cast additions such as Robert Patrick and Annabeth Gish, Mulder and Scully's replacements in the X-Files unit: John Doggett and Monica Reyes, respectively). This is not classic monster-of-the week stuff by a longshot and, as such, is disappointing even for a standalone story.
    and 3) It doesn't live up to the hopes of true X-Files fans who have clamored for a new movie for YEARS since it was first rumored.

    The good parts? Billy Connolly. The eccentric and funny Scottish actor/comedian (?) dours it up as a pedophile priest looking for salvation via his own psychic connection to the case Scully is asked to bring Mulder "out of the cold" on (ironically, it's snowing throughout the movie, so it's debatable how "out of the cold" he is working to get back into the good-graces of the FBI - did you remember they were after him for murder charges after the series ended in 2002?!? I barely did!)...

    Amanda Peet is not particularly bad as Dakota Whitney, the FBI agent who brings Mulder and Scully into contact with "Father Joe" (Connelly) in the first place.

    Apparently (here's what I know about the plot): Women (and men, though none are seen) are abducted along a roadside in Virginia and taken to a mysterious makeshift lab where they are locked in boxes and tested. Aliens? Conspirators from the government? Nope, just some eccentric Russkies who don't appear to know how to hide their true identities.

    If all of this sounds like a negative review (I just realized this), then think of it how a previous reviewer did: For fans of the series, rent it (maybe). Others, skip it.
